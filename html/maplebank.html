MapleBank

Application Design



About the Project

MapleBank is a fully online bank with no physical branches (a neobank) in Canada that is quickly gaining traction and is now looking to expand its market in the country.

The challenge for this project was to design an app focusing only on one functionality that would break into new banking markets across the country, targeting only new clients.

For this project, we were given information about current MapleBank users as well as possible new users (personas). We were also given information about their current portfolio of products.

The final product is a high fidelity prototype for the CIO of MapleBank

The Team: Alberto, Cristina, and me (Chelsea)

Timeline: 6 weeks

Our Roles: Since this was a student project, the roles were initially divided to provide everyone with equal learning opportunities in all stages of the design process. As the project progressed the roles evolved to better align with each member's individual strengths.


Brainstorming

To begin, we brainstormed many possible features we could introduce to MapleBank. We wanted to choose a product that would attract a new audience to MapleBank. We came up with many different ideas and we thought focusing on international transfers and rewards for completing challenges set by the bank would be best the best product.

We decided to focus on a mobile app since the data given to us showed 62% of users reply on their phone for banking purposes.

Competitive Analysis

We took a look at similar companies to identify their strengths, weaknesses and areas of opportunities. This helped us identify what was common in each app to know what was necessary to include but also different pain points each app had so we could avoid it or take a different approach.

Some of the insights gained were:

  • have an easy way to change currencies
  • be transparent with the fees
  • offer rewards for using the app
  • give an estimated time for how long it will take the money to reach the recipient


User Interviews

Although we were given potential personas by the bank, we still wanted to conduct some user interviews to understand what users were expecting from a banking app focused on foreign exchange.

Some of the insights gained were:
  • to show live conversion rates
  • to give a notification when the entire exchange has been completed
  • to give a notification when the conversion rate has dropped

KPIs

We wanted to focus on three key performace indicators to measure the performance of the new exchange feature we are introducing.

  • User Engagement: by monitoring the number of exchanges by active users

  • User Retention: by tracking the percentage of users who return to the app after the new feature

  • User Growth: by the increase in sign ups after the new feature

Persona

MapleBank provided us with some potential personas, we wanted to attract new users that exchange money abroad. We analyzed the given information and decided on Aya. Many of her characteristics differed from current MapleBank users, and we thought focusing on this persona would help us attract new clients.


Journey Map

We created a journey map that guided Aya's journey from logging in to sending money off to her family. This helped us to understand the pain points and the motions a user goes through to exchange money.

Low Fidelity Wireframes

Each of us developed a low fidelity wireframe on Figma that included the main feature of our app. This helped us understand each other's vision of the app as well as establish a list of requirements each page of the app should include.



Medium Fidelity Wireframe

We each developed medium fidelity wireframes which included potential ideas for colours and fonts. Going through this process individually helped us explore many different layout and colour schemes.

We started user testing at this stage to see if the flow and page layout made sense to users. We learned that there was a problem with the hierarchy of information on our main screen - the information most important to users wasn't standing out. We also found that the complementary colours, blue and yellow, was too overwhelming.



High Fidelity Wireframe

For our final product, we combined all of our design ideas.

We also addressed the problems that arose during user testing. We thought about the main focus of our app, exchanges and rewards, and allowed that to become the central focus of the main screen.

We used an analogous colour, a blue-teal, to replace important buttons with and to highlight parts of the app that we wanted to stand out.

User Testing

Functionality

Many iterations were needed to get to the product we have now. We started off by brainstorming the main features our app needed and then each of us created our own low fidelity wireframe. Then we tested on those wireframes, met up, and discussed the feedback from our users. We continued this process of designing and testing until we reached the product we have now. This helped us create a product that aligns to what a user like Aya would want and need.

User Interface (UI)

By creating multiple versions of the home screen with different colours, we were able to get feedback from user testing as to what colours we should use in our app.

In the end, we found that blue was the most favourable choice. Though once we made our choice, users found the app looked too monochrome - the important buttons didn’t stick out and everything just blended together. To fix this we found an analogous colour, a blue-teal, to replace important buttons with and to highlight parts of the app that we wanted to stand out.

The font, Montserrat Bold, was chosen by us as designers, as we thought this was a modern and very legible font.
